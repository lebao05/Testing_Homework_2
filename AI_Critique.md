During this assignment, AI was valuable for supporting the application of Domain Testing and Boundary Value Analysis. It quickly analyzed feature descriptions, identified possible input domains, suggested equivalence classes, and generated an initial set of test cases. This significantly reduced the time required to prepare testing documentation and provided a useful starting point for systematic analysis. However, the AI was not consistently accurate. In several cases, it assumed business rules that were not explicitly stated in the requirements, generated duplicate or unnecessary test cases, and occasionally missed important edge cases related to the actual implementation of the EShop application. For example, AI sometimes treated password validation rules or application-specific constraints as generic best practices instead of verifying them against the real system behavior. It also failed to identify certain bugs that only became apparent after executing the test cases on the running application.

These limitations occurred because AI primarily predicts likely responses based on patterns in its training data rather than reasoning directly from the application's implementation. The quality of its output also depended heavily on the clarity and specificity of the prompts provided. Ambiguous requirements or incomplete prompts often resulted in incomplete or incorrect test cases.

The most important lesson I learned is that AI should be used as a disciplined assistant rather than as an autonomous testing tool. Breaking the testing process into structured steps, reviewing every AI-generated result, validating assumptions against the system, and refining prompts iteratively produced much higher-quality outcomes than relying on a single generic prompt. Human judgment remains essential to ensure the correctness, completeness, and practical usefulness of the final testing artifacts.
